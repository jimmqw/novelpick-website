Books Like Solo Leveling — Top 15 Recommendations for fans of Chugwang's masterpiece

By NovelPick Editorial April 2026 Web Novel Recommendations

Introduction: Why Solo Leveling Captivated Millions

When Solo Leveling (by Chugwang, serialized on Kakao and later adapted by D&C Media) burst onto the web fiction scene, it redefined what a progression fantasy could achieve. The story of Sung Jin-Woo — the weakest hunter who discovers he alone can see dungeons hidden from everyone else, becoming the sole "Solo Player" with a system that lets him grow stronger through combat — resonated with readers for reasons that go beyond simple power fantasy.

Solo Leveling succeeded because it balanced tight pacing with escalating stakes, gave its protagonist genuine vulnerability and growth, and created a dungeon-crawling system that felt both game-like and genuinely dangerous. Its massive success (reportedly over 4.8 billion views on Kakao, a Netflix anime adaptation in 2024, and consistent rankings at the top of novel aggregation sites) spawned countless imitations, but few captured that same magic.

If you've finished Solo Leveling and are hungry for something that scratches that same itch — hunter systems, underdog protagonists, dungeon exploration, and the satisfaction of watching a character go from zero to unstoppable — this list is for you. We've curated 15 recommendations across Korean, Chinese, and Japanese web fiction that share Solo Leveling's DNA while offering their own unique twists.

How We Selected These Books

Our recommendations focus on web novels and light novels with active translations or completed fan translations. We prioritized stories that share at least two of Solo Leveling's key elements: a system-based progression mechanic, dungeon/instance content, an underdog-to-overdog protagonist arc, or a hunter/gate theme. Each recommendation includes why it resonates with Solo Leveling fans and what makes it distinctive.

Top 15 Recommendations

1. The Beginning After The End (by Turtleme)

If Solo Leveling got you hooked on progression fantasy, The Beginning After The End is the natural next step. King Grey has unrivaled beauty, power, and wealth — yet is cursed with the ugliest soul. Reborn into a magical world as a young mage, he resolves to rewrite his fate while uncovering the mysteries of this new life.

Like Solo Leveling, it features a protagonist who starts powerful in a past life and levels up again in a new world. The world-building is rich, the magic system is internally consistent, and the pacing keeps you turning pages. TBATE has one of the most active fan communities in the English web fiction space, with high-quality art, an ongoing Manhwa adaptation, and consistent chapter releases.

Why it's similar: Rebirth with a system, underdog start, rich cultivation/magic progression, dungeons and public quests

2. Second Life Ranker (by SadoYuna)

Channeling Solo Leveling's dual-life concept, Second Life Ranker follows a character whose father is killed by monsters from the Tower of Mirages. To avenge him, the protagonist uses an anomalous "Moon Sword" technique he found to enter the Tower — where players from Earth must climb floors, fight monsters, and level up to survive.

The Tower climbing mechanic mirrors Solo Leveling's dungeon raids, and the protagonist's zero-to-hero arc through the Tower's ranking system feels satisfyingly familiar. The story adds complexity with multiplayer raid elements and faction politics among climbers. The pacing is brisk and the fight scenes are visceral.

Why it's similar: Tower/dungeon climbing, ranking systems, hunt-and-level mechanics, revenge arc

3. Dungeon Predator (by K.S. Maslow)

Darius was a legendary gamer who dominated virtual reality MMOs — until a mysterious new game system called "Dungeon & Fighter Online" turns out to be real. When the game's monsters begin invading reality, he must use his in-game skills to protect humanity.

Dungeon Predator leans heavily into the game-like HUD and inventory systems that made Solo Leveling so satisfying. The protagonist's strategic approach to dungeon clearing — analyzing mechanics, optimizing loadouts — creates a different flavor of power fantasy compared to Jin-Woo's more combat-focused style. The story is completed with over 600 chapters of translated content.

Why it's similar: Game systems embedded in reality, dungeon mechanics, skill tree progression, hunter-protagonist

4. Legendary Moonlight Sculptor (by Nam Hee-Sung)

The classic that defined Korean web fiction for a generation. The story follows Weed, a reluctantly famous actor who retreats into the virtual reality MMO "Royal Road" to earn money for his family. What begins as financial necessity becomes an epic adventure as Weed's obsessive dedication to the game's crafting and combat systems makes him a legend.

While its tone is lighter than Solo Leveling and it focuses more on open-world MMO gameplay than dungeon raiding, the way Weed's systematic grinding and unconventional strategies overcome seemingly impossible challenges echoes Jin-Woo's methodical growth. The novel is completed with 580+ chapters, and its depth of game mechanics influenced virtually every Korean web fiction that followed.

Why it's similar: System-driven gaming world, methodical protagonist growth, grinding as a path to power, hunter/quest structure

5. Overlord (by Kugun Maruyama)

A dark, mature take on the trapped-in-a-game premise. When his favorite MMO "Yggdrasil" shuts down, longtime player Momonga refuses to log out. Instead, he finds himself reincarnated into the game's body of his max-level skeleton mage character — with all his party's NPC servants now sentient and loyal.

Overlord inverts Solo Leveling's underdog arc: Momonga is already one of the most powerful beings in the new world, and the tension comes from his monstrous faction's impact on innocent people and the political landscape. The character work is exceptional, the world-building is meticulous, and it offers a compelling counterpoint to Solo Leveling's "start weak, get strong" formula.

Why it's similar: Trapped in a game world, dungeon/siege battles, powerful protagonist with faction, dark fantasy tone

6. A Returner's Magic Should Be Special (by Usonan)

When the Shadow Labyrinth — the most dangerous dungeon in the world — wipes out humanity's strongest adventurers, mage Desir Arm-band is sent back in time 10 years. With knowledge of the future disaster, he must train the next generation of adventurers to be strong enough to survive it.

The time-reversal mechanic creates delicious tension as Desir uses foreknowledge to manipulate events, while his role as a teacher/trainer gives the story a different emotional texture from Solo Leveling's lone wolf. The magical academy setting and strategic group combat planning make this a favorite among readers who appreciated Solo Leveling's system mechanics but wanted more team-based content.

Why it's similar: System-like magic classification, group dungeons, underdog protagonist with hidden knowledge, escalating difficulty

7. Tomb Raider King (by Researchman)

Jinh妄 was the #1 Raider in the game "Dungeon Raid Online" — until a mysterious new Relic tomb appears and he logs in to find his character has been reset to Level 1. Now competing with raiders worldwide for control of these powerful Relic tombs, Jinh妄 must rebuild from nothing while the old power players underestimate him.

Tomb Raider King shares Solo Leveling's "start from zero" core mechanic and its emphasis on item/relic collection. The "Relic" system provides a collectible angle that appeals to the same players who enjoyed tracking Jin-Woo's Shadow Army growth. Fast-paced, action-heavy, and completed at over 500 chapters.

Why it's similar: Reset to Level 1, raiding dungeons for powerful items, underdog rankings, hunter/monster combat

8. Solo Leveling: The Novelization (by Chugwang)

This might seem obvious, but if you've only experienced Solo Leveling through the Manhwa adaptation, the original novel offers a deeper dive into Sung Jin-Woo's internal monologue, more detailed system mechanics, and side content the adaptation couldn't include. Sometimes the best follow-up to a story is more of that exact story.

The web novel is fully translated, and the supplemental content (character backstories, system explanations, extra chapters) rewards even dedicated Manhwa readers. If you're specifically looking for a second dose of Jin-Woo's journey before moving on, this delivers.

Why it's similar: Same story, deeper and more complete version, bonus content only in the original

9. Reincarnation of the Succubus (by Hyacinth)

A darker, more mature Solo Leveling-adjacent story. Succubus Queen Mana, one of the demon realm's most powerful beings, is reincarnated into a human infant on Earth just as dungeons have begun appearing and "Players" — humans who can hunt monsters — have emerged. Now starting over in a weak human body but retaining her memories and power-seeking nature, she must adapt her demon-realm strategies to this new reality.

Female protagonist, darker tone, and a fascinating fish-out-of-water angle as a powerful being navigates a weaker vessel. The dungeon mechanics are well-developed and the power-scaling is handled thoughtfully. For readers who want Solo Leveling's progression with a fresh perspective.

Why it's similar: System-class mechanics, dungeon hunting, regression to a weaker form, power through fighting dungeons

10. Omniscient Reader's Viewpoint (by Sing Shong)

Kim Dok-Ja has read the web novel "Three Ways to Survive a Ruined World" for 10 years. When the novel's apocalyptic scenarios begin occurring in real life — with monsters emerging from "Dungeons" and scenarios imposing deadly constraints on humanity — his encyclopedic knowledge of the novel becomes the key to survival.

ORV is consistently ranked among the best Korean web novels, with a protagonist whose power isn't physical but informational — he knows how the story goes, giving him strategic foresight no one else possesses. The Dokkaebi scenarios and constellation system create a game-like overlay that echoes Solo Leveling's system UI. The character work is phenomenal and the emotional payoff of its finale is unmatched in the genre.

Why it's similar: Game/scenario system, constellation sponsorship (like Jin-Woo's shadow soldiers), apocalypse with dungeons, strategic protagonist

11. The Novel's Extra (by J. B. Jin)

Kim Hajin wakes up in the world of a novel he wrote — as the novel's extra character who dies in the first chapter. Armed with meta-knowledge of the story's events, he must navigate a complex web of probability manipulation abilities, factions, and apocalyptic scenarios while desperately trying to avoid the fate he wrote for himself.

Another knowledge-reward story, but with a unique protagonist ability (probability manipulation through cards) and a focus on the gray morality that emerges when someone knows exactly how events will unfold. The pacing is excellent and the power system is one of the most creative in Korean web fiction. Completed at 800+ chapters.

Why it's similar: System-like ability classification, dungeon scenarios, meta-knowledge protagonist, underdog survival

12. Hardcore Leveling Warrior (by K.CH.

A satirical take on Korean web fiction power fantasy, following Warrior — a player so powerful he quits the game in disgust at how easy it is. But powerful NPCs want him back in the game, and Earth is about to face a threat only he can handle.

Self-aware and funny while still delivering satisfying action, Hardcore Leveling Warrior skewers the power fantasy tropes while indulging them. The "game system as reality" premise is played with rather than played straight. A good choice when you've had your fill of grim, serious Solo Leveling clones and want something that engages with the genre more playfully.

Why it's similar: Game mechanics in reality, powerful-protagonist-later, dungeon/siege content, meta-humor about progression fantasy

13. God of Crime (by Horseman)

Seong Jin-Woo — yes, a name that echoes Solo Leveling's protagonist — is a disgraced former hunter whose career ended in scandal. Working as a private investigator specializing in hunting-related crimes, he discovers a hidden dungeon with mysterious conditions that boost his abilities.

God of Crime is more grounded than Solo Leveling (no magical system UI, no rebirth), but it captures the dungeon exploration and monster-hunting thrill. The protagonist is older, more experienced, and his investigation/crime-solving approach to dungeons creates a procedural mystery layer that adds narrative texture. Completed at over 400 chapters with full fan translation.

Why it's similar: Dungeon hunting, hunter protagonist, monster combat, underdog recovery arc, Korean setting

14. I Am The Great Immortal (by Wu Yan Jin Mao)

A Chinese web novel where protagonist "Bei Zhou" discovers he was merely the strongest among his peers — not the strongest in the universe. Upon death and rebirth into a feudal cultivation world, he realizes that his previous realm's cultivation techniques are worthless here. He must start from the bottom in a world of immortals, demons, and sects.

Cultivation novels offer a different flavor of progression fantasy from Solo Leveling's game-like system, but the underlying appeal — watching a protagonist systematically overcome obstacles, level up through clearly defined stages, and grow from weak to unstoppable — is identical. I Am The Great Immortal is a complete, well-translated novel with strong pacing and a satisfying power arc.

Why it's similar: System-like cultivation stages, dungeon/secret realm exploration, reincarnation, grinding to power, clear level progression

15. Welcome to the Impregnable Demon King Castle (by Masamichi Otsuka)

A Japanese light novel about a young man named R上位 who is summoned to another world — not as a hero, but as the Demon King of a nearly destroyed castle. With no subjects, no army, and monsters constantly attacking, he must use his unique "Castle System" to rebuild his domain from nothing.

While the dungeon content is less prominent than in Solo Leveling, the "build from zero" and "system overlaying reality" elements will appeal to fans. The comedic tone and isekai twist on the "trapped in a game world" trope offer a lighter alternative. The series is completed at 11 volumes with a solid English translation.

Why it's similar: System-overlaid reality, building from nothing, monster/dungeon threats, underdog arc

The Verdict

Solo Leveling carved out a specific niche — the system-equipped hunter clearing dungeons alone, starting from absolute weakness — and these 15 recommendations each take elements of that formula in their own direction. Whether you want the deeper emotional journey of a complete Solo Leveling novel experience (Omniscient Reader's Viewpoint), the satisfying game mechanics of Second Life Ranker (tower climbing with rankings), or the power fantasy reversal of Overlord, there's something here.

If we had to pick a single recommendation: Omniscient Reader's Viewpoint. It has the same genre-defining quality Solo Leveling achieved, a protagonist whose meta-knowledge creates genuinely tense situations, and a narrative payoff that readers consistently rank among the most satisfying in all of web fiction. It's not the same as Solo Leveling — nothing is — but it's that good.

Whatever you choose, start with Chapter 1 rather than jumping in mid-series. These stories reward readers who give them time to establish their worlds. Happy reading — and happy hunting.
