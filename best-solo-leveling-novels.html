Best Solo Leveling Novels

📅 April 7, 2026⏰ 10 min read

Solo Leveling did something no series before it had quite managed �?it made the act of being the only one who can do something feel genuinely thrilling. Sung Jinwoo's solitary climb from the bottom of the hunter hierarchy to a realm no one else could access created a template that web fiction has been riffing on and refining ever since. If you have been chasing that same high �?the dungeon raid, the stat screen, the moment when everyone else panics but you are already three steps ahead �?this list was built for you.

These twelve recommendations span Korean web novels, Japanese light novels, Royal Road serials, and Western LitRPGs. What they share is the Solo Leveling DNA: hunter or dungeon systems, protagonists who start from a position of weakness or isolation, and the systematic, deeply satisfying mechanics of growing stronger one raid at a time.

The Solo Leveling Formula

Before diving in, it is worth acknowledging what makes Solo Leveling work so well as a framework. The core elements are: a clear numerical progression system, dungeons that function as both threat and reward, a protagonist whose isolation is both their weakness and �?eventually �?their defining strength, and a power fantasy that earns its victories through visible, quantifiable growth. Every novel on this list delivers some version of that formula, and the best ones find ways to surprise you within it.

1. Shadow Slave by biotran

Guilty Third �?the protagonist of Shadow Slave �?starts with a shadow class that everyone considers near-useless, and the first arc of this novel is a masterclass in making weakness feel agonizing before it transforms into overwhelming power. The dungeon mechanics are brutal, the shadow powers expand in genuinely surprising ways, and the psychological weight of the protagonist's situation gives the progression real stakes. Shadow Slave is arguably the closest thing to a direct Solo Leveling successor that exists �?it takes the same ingredients and executes them with even darker atmosphere and more complex world-building.

2. Super Gene Optimization Bath 9 by PhantomR

The premise is simple: a gene optimization system that improves the body one statistic at a time. What makes it compelling is the patience of the execution �?the protagonist does not become a god overnight. Each chapter brings a slightly better version of the same body, and the cumulative effect of watching him optimize himself past every natural limit is deeply satisfying in the same way that watching Sung Jinwoo clear dungeon after dungeon is satisfying. The power ceiling is absurdly high, and the novel knows it.

Royal Road | Ongoing | Western LitRPG

3. Defiance of the Fall by JF Brink

A System integration event transforms Earth into a multiverse of dungeons, rifts, and monsters �?and the protagonist is among the first to receive a combat-oriented class in a world where most players get support or crafting roles. Where Solo Leveling is intimate and personal in its dungeon encounters, Defiance of the Fall scales outward to global catastrophe, faction politics, and dimensional threats. The power fantasy is bigger and more brutal, and the protagonist's ruthlessness in exploiting system mechanics feels like an evolution of the lone-hunter concept.

Western LitRPG

Amazon | 2023 | Ongoing | Western LitRPG

4. Dungeon Crawler Carl by Matt Dinniman

Earth is being used as a procedurally-generated dungeon crawler reality show by alien audiences, and the protagonist �?Carl �?is one of the survivors fighting through increasingly bizarre and deadly levels. The premise sounds like comedy, and it is funny, but the dungeon mechanics are deeply serious and the stakes are visceral. The system's complexity and the protagonist's ability to think outside the box within it will remind Solo Leveling fans of the strategic pleasure of watching a protagonist exploit rules others miss. The humor is a feature, not a bug �?it makes the dark moments land harder.

Western LitRPG

Royal Road | Ongoing | Western LitRPG

5. Primal Hunter by Zogarth

A gamer reincarnated into a world where hunting monsters is how people level up receives a Hunter class that everyone considers mediocre at best. The catch: no one has ever invested fully in it, so its hidden compounding mechanics are completely undocumented. What follows is part survival LitRPG, part power fantasy of optimization �?watching the protagonist build a character build that the established community dismissed is deeply pleasurable if you have ever played an RPG and figured out a non-standard path to victory.

Western LitRPG

Shōsetsuka ni Narō | Ongoing | Japanese Web Novel

6. Mushoku Tensei by Rifujin na Magonote

A 34-year-old shut-in is reincarnated into a fantasy world as Rudeus Greyrat with full memories and begins a systematic, decades-long process of mastering magic and swordsmanship. The pacing is slower than Solo Leveling and the power fantasy is more grounded, but the systematic magic training and the long-term character development have a similar satisfaction for readers who appreciate methodical growth. This is the gold standard of the Japanese isekai reincarnation genre and the direct ancestor of much of what Solo Leveling draws from.

7. The Legendary Mechanic by Chocolion

The protagonist transmigrates into a game world as an NPC instead of a player character �?which means he knows the future of the storyline, has access to mechanic skills that players would need years to develop, and can essentially play the game before it officially begins. The mechanic class is genuinely unique in LitRPG fiction, and the strategic pleasure of watching someone optimize a system from the inside while everyone else bumbles through it blind is a variation on the Solo Leveling formula that rewards intelligence over brute force.

8. Lord of the Mysteries by Cuttlefish That Loves Diving

Klein Moretti wakes up in a world modeled on Victorian-era occultism with a tarot-deck-based system of potions and sequential advancement through distinct categories. The pacing is deliberate, the mystery is genuinely complex, and the protagonist's isolation �?he cannot tell anyone about his system or his true identity �?echoes the lone-wolf quality of Solo Leveling. The tarot imagery and Victorian setting give it a very different flavor, but the dungeon equivalents (mystery sequences and confrontations) deliver the same satisfaction of watching someone play a game nobody else understands.

9. Overlord by Kugane Maruyama

Yggio �?an immortal skeleton MMO villain �?finds himself transported into his own game world with his full set of max-level equipment and an NPC servant faction. Where Solo Leveling's protagonist grows from weakest to strongest within a world's system, Overlord's protagonist is already the apex predator and the tension comes from his growing moral horror at what he has become. It is a fascinating inversion of the power fantasy, and the systematic nature of the game mechanics is handled with surprising nuance. If you want the dungeon-crawler dungeon-raider fantasy but from the other side of the power curve, this is essential.

10. Return of the Mount Hua Sect by Biga | translated

A martial arts prodigy who was expelled from his sect for destroying it through excessive talent is sent 150 years into the future where the sect he destroyed has become a legend. The dungeon equivalent here is the cultivation world's mountain dungeons, and the systematic tier-based advancement of the martial arts framework satisfies the same progression itch as Solo Leveling. The protagonist's return as the sole survivor of a legendary sect gives him the same lone-wolf dynamic as Sung Jinwoo �?everyone underestimates him, and the revelation of his actual level never gets old.

Royal Road | Ongoing | Western LitRPG

11.Infinite Stuck by SeventhStar

Trapped in a time loop where only the protagonist retains memories of previous iterations, each loop is both a dungeon and a training ground �?he spends loop after loop optimizing his build, uncovering hidden mechanics, and preparing for a boss fight that will otherwise kill everyone. The dungeon here is time itself, and the systematic nature of his progression within the loop creates a satisfaction that is distinct from Solo Leveling but deeply related: watching someone understand a system so completely that they can exploit it is one of the genre's purest pleasures.

12. All the Skills by Namo

A deck-building LitRPG where every skill drawn from the system is a permanent, irreversible choice �?no respec, no do-overs. The protagonist is a former competitive card game player whose analytical approach to character building translates directly into an edge over characters who choose skills based on convention rather than synergy. All the Skills distills the Solo Leveling progression fantasy into a compact three-book series that delivers everything the genre promises: a system with clear rules, a protagonist who understands those rules better than anyone else, and victories that feel earned through intelligence rather than luck.

The Solo Leveling Legacy

Solo Leveling did not invent the dungeon-crawler power fantasy, but it crystallized it into a form that web fiction has been riffing on productively ever since. The 12 novels on this list represent the best iterations of that template �?whether you want the darker Korean dungeon aesthetic, the lighter Japanese isekai tradition, the strategic depth of Western LitRPG, or the massive scale of Chinese web novels. The formula works because it is fundamentally about the pleasure of watching someone get better at something, and each of these books finds a way to make that pleasure feel fresh.

Our Verdict

The closest read to Solo Leveling: Shadow Slave �?same Korean web novel DNA, same dungeon aesthetic, darker tone. For systematic optimization fans: All the Skills and Primal Hunter reward the analytical reader who wants to see a protagonist exploit a system from the inside. For Japanese light novel lovers: Mushoku Tensei is the slow-burn masterpiece of the reincarnation genre. For big-scale dungeon crawlers: Dungeon Crawler Carl delivers absurdity and tension in equal measure. For mystery lovers: Lord of the Mysteries reframes the dungeon-crawler template through Victorian occultism and genuinely complex puzzle-solving.
